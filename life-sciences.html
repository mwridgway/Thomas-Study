DNA Structure & Replication

Structure of the Cell

The Nucleus

The nucleus contains the nucleolus, chromatin network, nuclear membrane, nucleoplasm, and nuclear pores.

Functions of the Nucleus

  • Chromosomes carry genetic material.
  • Regulates the structure and metabolism of the cell.

Nucleic Acids

  • Nucleic acids are organic molecules.
  • Functions: Protein synthesis; store and transfer genetic information.
  • Two types: DNA and RNA.

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)

Location:

  • Nuclear DNA: Found in the nucleus (consists of genes and chromosomes).
  • Extranuclear DNA: Small amounts found outside the nucleus in mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Chromosomes & Genes

  • Chromosomes are made of DNA wrapped around proteins called Histones.
  • Gene: A short segment of DNA that carries a code for the synthesis of specific proteins.
  • Proteins determine the characteristics (structure and function) of an organism.
  • Chromosomes are only visible in a dividing cell (not during interphase).

Structure of DNA

  • Huge polymer molecule made of monomers called Nucleotides.
  • Shape: Double Helix (unwound, it looks like a ladder).

The Nucleotide (3 Parts)

  1. Phosphate group (P)
  2. Sugar molecule (Deoxyribose)
  3. Nitrogenous Base:
    • Adenine (A) — Large (purine)
    • Guanine (G) — Large (purine)
    • Cytosine (C) — Small (pyrimidine)
    • Thymine (T) — Small (pyrimidine)

Bonding & Base Pairing

The DNA Ladder

  • Sides of ladder: Alternating Deoxyribose sugar and Phosphate groups (sugar-phosphate backbone).
  • Rungs of ladder: Base pairs joined by weak hydrogen bonds.

Base Pairing Rule

A — T

Adenine always pairs with Thymine

G — C

Guanine always pairs with Cytosine

Role of DNA

  • Genes carry genetic code.
  • The nitrogen base sequence determines the type of amino acids for protein formation.
  • Non-coding DNA: 98% of DNA does not carry information to form proteins.
  • Only 2% is coding DNA (carries the instructions for protein synthesis).

DNA Replication

Definition

Duplication of the DNA molecule to form two identical copies.

Timing: Occurs during Interphase.

The Process

  1. The double helix DNA unwinds.
  2. The double-stranded DNA unzips (weak hydrogen bonds break) to form two separate strands.
  3. The original strand serves as a template.
  4. Free-floating DNA nucleotides in the nucleoplasm form a complementary strand by attaching to the correct base partners (A-T, G-C).
  5. Two identical DNA copies are formed.

Importance of Replication

  • Essential before cell division (mitosis).
  • Ensures each daughter cell possesses the same genetic composition as the mother cell.

DNA Profiles & RNA

DNA Profiling

  • Scientists extract DNA, prepare and process it to obtain a barcode pattern.
  • 99% of human DNA is identical. Forensics uses the 1% non-coding DNA that differs between individuals.
  • Profiles are unique to individuals (except identical twins).
  • Sources: Extracted from skin, blood, semen, or hair found at a crime scene.

Uses of DNA Profiles

  • Identifying potential criminal suspects.
  • Paternity testing.
  • Identifying genetic disorders.
  • Identifying missing persons or unrecognisable bodies.
  • Compatibility of tissue/organs for transplants.

Interpreting DNA Profiles

Case 1: Paternity Testing

Compare the Mother, Child, and potential fathers.

Rule: If all bands in the child's profile that do not match the mother do match a specific man, he is the biological father.

Case 2: Criminal Suspects

Compare DNA from the crime scene (e.g., hair sample) to suspects.

Rule: An exact match links the suspect to the scene.

Case 3: Identifying Relatives

  • Identical twins: Have identical DNA profiles.
  • Non-identical twins: Have different profiles.

RNA (Ribonucleic Acid)

  • Location: Nucleus, Cytoplasm, Ribosome.
  • Structure: Single strand. Polymer made of nucleotides.

RNA Nucleotide Components

  • Sugar: Ribose (R)
  • Phosphate: (P)
  • Nitrogenous Bases: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), Uracil (U)
Key difference: RNA contains Uracil instead of Thymine.

Types of RNA

1. Messenger RNA (mRNA)

  • Structure: Single strand.
  • Location: Formed in the nucleoplasm using DNA as a template (Transcription).
  • Function: Carries the genetic code (triplets called Codons) from the DNA in the nucleus to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm. Acts as a messenger.

2. Transfer RNA (tRNA)

  • Structure: Single strand folded back on itself (clover/hairpin shape).
  • Feature: Has three exposed bases called an Anticodon.
  • Location: Cytoplasm.
  • Function: Picks up specific amino acids in the cytoplasm and carries them to the ribosome for protein synthesis.

Meiosis

What is Meiosis?

  • A type of cell division that produces gametes (sex cells).
  • Occurs only in the gonads (ovaries and testes).
  • One diploid (2n) cell → four haploid (n) daughter cells.
  • Involves two successive divisions: Meiosis I and Meiosis II.
  • Daughter cells are genetically different from each other and from the parent cell.

Why is Meiosis Important?

  • Produces haploid gametes — ensures chromosome number is maintained after fertilisation (n + n = 2n).
  • Creates genetic variation — essential for natural selection and evolution.

Meiosis I — The Reduction Division

Prophase I

  • Chromosomes shorten and thicken (condense).
  • Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) to form bivalents.
  • Crossing over occurs — non-sister chromatids exchange DNA at chiasmata.
  • Nuclear membrane and nucleolus disappear.
  • Centrioles move to opposite poles; spindle fibres form.

Metaphase I

  • Bivalents line up at the cell equator (metaphase plate).
  • Orientation of each pair is random (independent assortment).
  • Spindle fibres attach to centromeres.

Anaphase I

  • Homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles.
  • Centromeres do NOT split — each chromosome still has 2 sister chromatids.
  • This is the reduction division — chromosome number halved.

Telophase I & Cytokinesis

  • Chromosomes reach the poles.
  • Nuclear membranes may reform.
  • Cell divides → two haploid daughter cells.
  • A short interkinesis (rest) may follow — NO DNA replication.

Meiosis II — Similar to Mitosis

Prophase II

Chromosomes condense. Nuclear membrane disappears. Spindle fibres form.

Metaphase II

Individual chromosomes (not bivalents) line up at the equator.

Anaphase II

  • Centromeres split.
  • Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.

Telophase II & Cytokinesis

  • Nuclear membranes reform.
  • Cytokinesis occurs.
  • Result: 4 genetically unique haploid daughter cells.

How Meiosis Creates Genetic Variation

1. Crossing Over

During Prophase I, non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange DNA at chiasmata. Creates new allele combinations on chromosomes.

2. Independent Assortment

During Metaphase I, bivalents line up randomly. Each gamete gets a unique mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes. With 23 pairs: 2²³ = 8,388,608 combinations!

3. Random Fertilisation

Any sperm can fuse with any egg. This further multiplies genetic variation.

All three mechanisms together produce virtually unlimited genetic diversity — the raw material for natural selection and evolution.

Abnormal Meiosis — Non-disjunction

What is Non-disjunction?

  • The failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis.
  • Can occur in meiosis I (homologous chromosomes don't separate) or meiosis II (sister chromatids don't separate).
  • Results in gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers: n+1 or n-1.

Consequences

  • After fertilisation: trisomy (2n+1 = 47 chromosomes) or monosomy (2n-1 = 45 chromosomes).
  • Down syndrome (Trisomy 21): three copies of chromosome 21 → 47 chromosomes total.

Karyotype

A karyotype is a photograph of chromosomes arranged in homologous pairs (largest to smallest). Used to detect abnormalities such as Down syndrome or Turner syndrome (missing X chromosome).

Plant Hormones

What are Plant Hormones?

  • Plant hormones (phytohormones) are chemical substances produced in small quantities in one part of the plant and transported to another part where they have an effect.
  • They regulate plant growth, development, and responses to environmental stimuli.
  • Unlike animal hormones, plant hormones are not produced in specific glands.
  • The five main plant hormones: Auxins, Gibberellins, Abscisic acid, Cytokinins, and Ethylene.

Key Terms

  • Tropism: A directional growth response of a plant towards or away from a stimulus.
  • Positive tropism: Growth towards the stimulus.
  • Negative tropism: Growth away from the stimulus.
  • Phototropism: Growth response to light.
  • Geotropism (gravitropism): Growth response to gravity.

Auxins

Production & Transport

  • Produced in the apical meristems (tips of shoots and roots).
  • Transported away from the tip (basipetally — from top to bottom).
  • Main natural auxin: Indole-3-acetic acid (IAA).

Functions of Auxins

  • Cell elongation: Stimulates elongation of cells in the shoot.
  • Phototropism: Auxin moves to the shaded side of the shoot, causing cells there to elongate more → shoot bends towards light (positive phototropism).
  • Geotropism in shoots: In a horizontal shoot, auxin accumulates on the lower side, causing more elongation → shoot grows upward (negative geotropism).
  • Geotropism in roots: Auxin accumulates on the lower side, but roots are sensitive to high auxin concentrations — growth is inhibited on the lower side → root grows downward (positive geotropism).
  • Apical dominance: The apical bud produces auxin that inhibits growth of lateral (side) buds. Removing the tip removes auxin → lateral buds grow → bushier plant.
Key concept: Auxin has opposite effects in shoots vs roots at the same concentration. Shoots are stimulated; roots are inhibited.

Gibberellins

Production & Functions

  • Produced in young leaves, roots, and developing seeds.
  • Stem elongation: Promotes elongation of internodes (the space between nodes on a stem).
  • Seed germination: Triggers the production of enzymes that break down stored food in the seed → provides energy for the growing embryo.
  • Bolting: Rapid elongation of the stem before flowering in some plants (e.g., cabbage).
  • Fruit development: Can stimulate fruit growth.

Gibberellins vs Auxins

  • Gibberellins promote stem elongation (internode lengthening), while auxins promote cell elongation at the tip.
  • Gibberellins do not cause tropisms.

Abscisic Acid (ABA)

Production & Functions

  • Produced in mature leaves, stems, and roots.
  • Known as the "stress hormone" — helps the plant cope with unfavourable conditions.
  • Stomatal closure: Causes guard cells to close stomata during water stress/drought → reduces water loss by transpiration.
  • Seed dormancy: Maintains seeds in a dormant state, preventing germination during unfavourable conditions.
  • Bud dormancy: Inhibits growth of buds during winter.
  • Leaf abscission: Promotes the dropping of leaves in some species.
ABA is an inhibitory hormone — it generally slows down or stops growth processes. It opposes the effects of gibberellins and auxins.

Cytokinins

Production & Functions

  • Produced mainly in the roots and transported upward in the xylem.
  • Cell division: Stimulate cytokinesis (cell division), especially in the presence of auxins.
  • Delay senescence (ageing): Slow down the ageing and death of leaves and other plant organs → keep leaves green longer.
  • Promote lateral bud growth: Counteract apical dominance by encouraging side branches to grow.
  • Promote shoot growth in tissue culture: Used with auxins to control differentiation.

Cytokinins vs Auxins

  • Auxins promote apical dominance (suppress lateral buds); cytokinins counteract this by promoting lateral bud growth.
  • The ratio of auxin to cytokinin determines whether roots or shoots develop in tissue culture.

Ethylene

Production & Properties

  • Ethylene is the only plant hormone that is a gas.
  • Produced in ripening fruits, ageing flowers, and damaged/stressed tissues.

Functions

  • Fruit ripening: Stimulates the ripening process. One ripe fruit produces ethylene that triggers ripening in nearby fruits (chain reaction).
  • Abscission: Promotes the dropping of leaves, flowers, and fruits (leaf fall in autumn).
  • Senescence: Accelerates ageing of plant organs.
  • Triple response: In seedlings — inhibits stem elongation, promotes lateral growth (thickening), and causes horizontal growth.
Practical tip: Placing a ripe banana with unripe avocados speeds up ripening — the banana releases ethylene gas!

Classic Plant Hormone Experiments

Charles Darwin & Francis Darwin (1880)

  • Experimented with grass coleoptiles (protective sheath around emerging shoot).
  • Found that the tip of the coleoptile is responsible for detecting light.
  • When the tip was removed → no bending towards light.
  • When the tip was covered with an opaque cap → no bending.
  • When the base was covered but the tip exposed → bending still occurred.
  • Conclusion: The tip detects light, and a signal (later identified as auxin) travels from the tip to the elongation zone below to cause bending.

Boysen-Jensen (1913)

  • Cut the tip off a coleoptile and placed a gelatin block between the tip and stump → bending still occurred.
  • Placed a mica sheet (impermeable barrier) between tip and stump → no bending.
  • Conclusion: The signal from the tip is a chemical substance (not electrical) that can diffuse through gelatin but not through mica.

Fritz Went (1928)

  • Removed coleoptile tips and placed them on agar blocks to collect the chemical.
  • Placed the agar block off-centre on a decapitated coleoptile (in the dark) → the coleoptile bent away from the side with the agar block.
  • The degree of bending was proportional to the amount of chemical in the agar.
  • Named the chemical "auxin" (from Greek "auxein" = to grow).
  • Conclusion: Auxin is the chemical that causes cell elongation. Unequal distribution of auxin causes bending.

Summary of Experiments

Darwin1880The tip of the coleoptile detects light and sends a signal downward
Boysen-Jensen1913The signal is a chemical (diffuses through gelatin, not mica)

Commercial Uses of Plant Hormones

Auxins

  • Rooting powder: Synthetic auxins promote root growth in stem cuttings.
  • Herbicides: Synthetic auxins (e.g., 2,4-D) used as selective weedkillers — kill broadleaf weeds but not grasses.
  • Seedless fruit: Auxin application can produce seedless (parthenocarpic) fruits.
  • Prevent fruit drop: Auxin sprays keep fruit on trees until harvest.

Gibberellins

  • Increase fruit size: Applied to grapes to produce larger, seedless grapes.
  • Break seed dormancy: Used in brewing to speed up malting (barley germination).
  • Promote bolting: Used to increase stem length in sugar cane.

Cytokinins

  • Tissue culture: Used with auxins to grow plants from small tissue samples.
  • Delay senescence: Keep cut flowers and harvested vegetables fresh longer.

Ethylene

  • Ripen fruit artificially: Bananas and tomatoes are often picked green and ripened with ethylene gas during transport.
  • Synchronise pineapple flowering: Used to make all plants flower and fruit at the same time for efficient harvesting.
  • Promote latex flow: Applied to rubber trees to increase latex production.

Abscisic Acid

  • Anti-transpirant sprays: Can reduce water loss in transplanted crops.
  • Maintain dormancy: Used to keep stored potatoes from sprouting.
