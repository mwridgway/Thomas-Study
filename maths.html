Functions & Inverses

What is a Function?

  • A function is a relation where every input (x) maps to exactly one output (y).
  • Use the vertical line test: if any vertical line cuts the graph more than once, it is NOT a function.

What is an Inverse?

  • The inverse of function f, written f⁻¹, is obtained by swapping x and y.
  • If f(a) = b, then f⁻¹(b) = a.
  • Graphically: the inverse is the reflection in the line y = x.
  • The domain of f becomes the range of f⁻¹, and vice versa.

One-to-One Functions

  • A function is one-to-one if each y-value corresponds to only one x-value.
  • Use the horizontal line test: if any horizontal line cuts the graph more than once, the function is NOT one-to-one.
  • Only one-to-one functions have inverses that are also functions (without restriction).

Inverse of a Linear Function

Finding the Inverse of y = ax + q

  1. Swap x and y: x = ay + q
  2. Solve for y: y = (x − q)/a

The inverse of a linear function is always a function (linear functions are one-to-one).

Example

f(x) = 2x + 4

  • Swap: x = 2y + 4
  • Solve: y = (x − 4)/2 = ½x − 2
  • f⁻¹(x) = ½x − 2

Graphical Properties

  • f and f⁻¹ are reflections of each other in y = x.
  • They intersect on the line y = x.
  • The gradient of f⁻¹ is the reciprocal of the gradient of f.

Inverse of a Quadratic Function (Parabola)

y = ax² (parabola with vertex at origin)

  1. Swap: x = ay²
  2. Solve: y = ±√(x/a)

This is NOT a function because each x gives two y-values (± square root). The graph is a sideways parabola.

Restricting the Domain

To make the inverse a function, restrict the domain of the original parabola:

  • x ≥ 0 (right half): inverse is y = √(x/a) — top branch
  • x ≤ 0 (left half): inverse is y = −√(x/a) — bottom branch

Diagram Description: Parabola and Its Inverse

Original: y = x² is a U-shaped parabola opening upward, vertex at (0,0), passing through (1,1), (2,4), (−1,1), (−2,4).

Inverse (unrestricted): x = y² is a sideways parabola opening right, passing through (1,1), (1,−1), (4,2), (4,−2).

Line y = x: The diagonal line through origin at 45°. The original and inverse are mirror images across this line.

Restricted inverse (x ≥ 0): Only the top branch: y = √x, passing through (0,0), (1,1), (4,2).

Exponential & Logarithmic Functions

Exponential Function: y = bˣ (b > 0, b ≠ 1)

  • Domain: x ∈ ℝ
  • Range: y > 0
  • Asymptote: y = 0 (x-axis)
  • y-intercept: (0, 1) — because b⁰ = 1
  • If b > 1: graph increases (growth). If 0 < b < 1: graph decreases (decay).

Finding the Inverse

  1. Swap: x = bʸ
  2. Rewrite in log form: y = log_b(x)

The inverse is always a function — exponentials are one-to-one.

Logarithmic Function: y = log_b(x)

  • Domain: x > 0
  • Range: y ∈ ℝ
  • Asymptote: x = 0 (y-axis)
  • x-intercept: (1, 0) — because log_b(1) = 0
  • Passes through (b, 1) — because log_b(b) = 1

Key Relationship

  • log_b(bˣ) = x and b^(log_b(x)) = x — they undo each other.
  • The domains and ranges swap between the exponential and its log inverse.

Diagram Description: Exponential and Log Graphs

y = 2ˣ: Curve through (0,1), (1,2), (2,4), (−1,0.5), approaching y = 0 on the left.

y = log₂(x): Curve through (1,0), (2,1), (4,2), (0.5,−1), approaching x = 0 from the right.

y = x: Both graphs are reflections across this line.

Sketching Inverses & Reading from Graphs

Steps to Sketch an Inverse

  1. Identify key points on the original (intercepts, turning points, asymptote points).
  2. Swap x and y coordinates: (a, b) → (b, a).
  3. Plot the new points and draw the curve.
  4. Draw the line y = x to check the reflection.

Reading from Graphs

  • If given the graph of f, you can read f⁻¹(a) by finding where y = a on the graph of f, then the corresponding x-value is f⁻¹(a).
  • f and f⁻¹ intersect on the line y = x.
  • To find intersection points, solve f(x) = x.

Euclidean Geometry

The Proportionality Theorem

Theorem Statement

A line drawn parallel to one side of a triangle divides the other two sides proportionally.

If DE ∥ BC in △ABC (D on AB, E on AC), then: AD/DB = AE/EC

Also: AD/AB = AE/AC and DB/AB = EC/AC.

Diagram Description

△ABC with A at top, B bottom-left, C bottom-right. Point D is on AB between A and B. Point E is on AC between A and C. Line DE is drawn parallel to BC. The small triangle △ADE sits inside the larger △ABC.

Proof (Examinable)

Given: △ABC with DE ∥ BC, D on AB, E on AC.

Required to prove: AD/DB = AE/EC

Construction: Draw heights h₁ from E perpendicular to AB, and h₂ from D perpendicular to AC. Join BE and CD.

  1. Area(△ADE)/Area(△BDE) = (½ · AD · h₁)/(½ · DB · h₁) = AD/DB ... (same height h₁ from E)
  2. Area(△ADE)/Area(△CDE) = (½ · AE · h₂)/(½ · EC · h₂) = AE/EC ... (same height h₂ from D)
  3. But △BDE and △CDE have the same base DE and lie between parallel lines DE and BC, so they have equal areas.
  4. Therefore Area(△ADE)/Area(△BDE) = Area(△ADE)/Area(△CDE)
  5. Therefore AD/DB = AE/EC. ∎

Converse

If a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same proportion, then that line is parallel to the third side.

The Midpoint Theorem

Theorem

The segment joining the midpoints of two sides of a triangle is:

  • Parallel to the third side, and
  • Equal to half its length.

If D is the midpoint of AB and E is the midpoint of AC, then DE ∥ BC and DE = ½BC.

Converse

A line drawn through the midpoint of one side of a triangle, parallel to another side, bisects the third side.

Diagram Description

△ABC. D is the midpoint of AB (AD = DB). E is the midpoint of AC (AE = EC). Line DE is parallel to BC. DE = ½BC.

Similar Triangles

Definition

Two triangles are similar (|||) if they have the same shape (but not necessarily the same size).

Conditions for Similarity

  1. AAA (Angle-Angle-Angle): All corresponding angles are equal. (In practice, you only need to prove 2 angles equal since the third follows.)
  2. SSS (proportional sides): All corresponding sides are in the same proportion.

Theorem: Equiangular Triangles Have Proportional Sides

If two triangles are equiangular, then their corresponding sides are proportional.

If △ABC ||| △DEF, then AB/DE = BC/EF = AC/DF.

Converse

If the corresponding sides of two triangles are proportional, then the triangles are equiangular (and therefore similar).

Writing Similarity Statements

Always list vertices in corresponding order. If Â = Đ, Ŷ = Ê, Ĉ = Ŷ, write: △ABC ||| △DEF.

Area Ratio

If two similar triangles have sides in ratio k:1, their areas are in ratio k²:1.

Triangles with Same Base Between Parallel Lines

Triangles on the same base and between the same parallel lines have equal areas (same base, same height).

Theorem of Pythagoras — Proof Using Similar Triangles

Theorem

In a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides: AB² = AC² + BC².

Diagram Description

△ABC with the right angle at C. AB is the hypotenuse. The altitude CD is drawn from C perpendicular to AB, meeting AB at D. This creates two smaller triangles: △ACD (left) and △BCD (right).

Proof (Examinable)

Given: △ABC with Ĉ = 90°.

Construction: Draw CD ⊥ AB (altitude from C to hypotenuse).

Step 1: Show △ACD ||| △ABC

  • In △ACD and △ABC:
  • Â is common
  • AD̂C = AĈB = 90°
  • Therefore △ACD ||| △ABC (AA)
  • So: AC/AB = AD/AC → AC² = AD · AB ... (i)

Step 2: Show △BCD ||| △ABC

  • In △BCD and △ABC:
  • Ŷ is common
  • BD̂C = BĈA = 90°
  • Therefore △BCD ||| △ABC (AA)
  • So: BC/AB = BD/BC → BC² = BD · AB ... (ii)

Step 3: Add (i) and (ii)

  • AC² + BC² = AD · AB + BD · AB
  • = AB(AD + BD)
  • = AB · AB
  • = AB²

Therefore AC² + BC² = AB². ∎

Converse

If the square of one side of a triangle equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides, then the angle opposite the longest side is 90°.

Circle Geometry

Central Angle & Inscribed Angle Theorems

Theorem 1: Angle at the Centre = 2 × Angle at Circumference

The angle subtended by an arc at the centre of a circle is twice the angle subtended by the same arc at any point on the circumference (on the same side of the chord).

Diagram: Circle with centre O. Points A and B on circumference define an arc. Point C on circumference (same side). AÔB (central angle) = 2 × AĈB (inscribed angle).

Theorem 2: Angle in a Semicircle = 90°

The angle subtended by a diameter at the circumference is always 90°.

Special case: The central angle for a diameter is 180°, so the inscribed angle = 180°/2 = 90°.

Theorem 3: Angles in the Same Segment Are Equal

Angles subtended by the same chord (or arc) at the circumference, on the same side of the chord, are equal.

Diagram: Circle with chord PQ. Points A and B are both on the major arc. PÂQ = PŶQ (both subtend the same arc PQ).

Converse

If a line segment subtends equal angles at two points on the same side, then the four points are concyclic (lie on a circle).

Cyclic Quadrilaterals

Definition

A cyclic quadrilateral has all four vertices on the circumference of a circle.

Theorem: Opposite Angles Are Supplementary

The opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral add up to 180°.

If ABCD is cyclic: Â + Ĉ = 180° and Ŷ + Đ = 180°.

Proof outline: Â is subtended by arc BCD, and Ĉ is subtended by arc BAD. Together, arcs BCD and BAD make the full circle (360°). The central angles sum to 360°, so the inscribed angles sum to 180°.

Converse

If the opposite angles of a quadrilateral are supplementary, the quadrilateral is cyclic.

Exterior Angle Theorem

An exterior angle of a cyclic quadrilateral equals the interior opposite angle.

How to Prove a Quadrilateral is Cyclic

  1. Show opposite angles are supplementary (sum to 180°).
  2. Show an exterior angle equals the interior opposite angle.
  3. Show two points subtend equal angles to a line segment (same segment theorem converse).

Tangent Theorems

Tangent ⊥ Radius

A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius drawn to the point of tangency. The angle between them is always 90°.

Two Tangents from an External Point

If two tangents are drawn from the same external point to a circle, then:

  • The tangent segments are equal in length.
  • The line from the external point to the centre bisects the angle between the tangents.

Diagram: External point P. Two tangent lines from P touch the circle at A and B. PA = PB. Line PO (O = centre) bisects angle APB.

Tangent-Chord (Tan-Chord) Theorem

The angle between a tangent and a chord drawn from the point of tangency equals the inscribed angle subtended by the chord in the alternate segment.

Diagram: Circle with tangent line at point A. Chord AB is drawn. The angle between the tangent and AB (on one side) equals the inscribed angle ACB where C is any point on the arc on the opposite side of AB.

This is one of the most useful theorems — look for tangent lines touching a circle and chords from that point.

Chord Properties

Perpendicular from Centre Bisects the Chord

A line drawn from the centre of a circle perpendicular to a chord bisects (cuts in half) that chord.

Converse: A line from the centre to the midpoint of a chord is perpendicular to the chord.

Equal Chords Subtend Equal Angles

If two chords are equal in length, they subtend equal angles at the centre.

Converse: If two chords subtend equal angles at the centre, the chords are equal.

Equal Chords Are Equidistant from the Centre

Equal chords in a circle are the same distance from the centre. The converse also holds.
