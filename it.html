Module 1.1 — Computers in Our Everyday Lives

Why We Use Computers

Three Main Reasons

The reasons for using computers can be summarised in three words: efficiency, accuracy and reliability. A fourth reason is communication.

Efficiency — Saving Time, Labour & Resources

  • Time: Computers work faster than humans. Some tasks need real-time control (e.g. spacecraft landing) that only computers can handle.
  • Labour: Computers reduce mentally intensive tasks (replacing some accountant/mathematician work) and repetitive physical tasks (robots work 24/7). They can also do dangerous tasks (e.g. bomb disposal robots).
  • Resources: Sensors in buildings switch lights and AC on/off automatically, saving electricity. Technology monitors carbon emissions in factories and cars.

Accuracy

  • Computers are extremely accurate as long as they have quality programs and quality data.
  • Repeatable accuracy is essential in mass production (e.g. assembly line welds must always be perfect).
  • If the software is correct, the computer can perform the same task with 100% accuracy every time.

Reliability

  • Computers can be relied on to do tasks accurately, without tiring, getting bored, complaining or asking for more money.
  • Initial setup is time-consuming and expensive, but in the long run, computerised solutions are more cost-effective.
Remember: Accuracy = correctness of output (needs good software + good data). Reliability = consistency without fatigue. These are related but distinct concepts.

Communication

How ICT Enables Communication

The efficiency, accuracy and reliability of computers, combined with communication ability, enable us to:

  • Virtually eliminate time and distance as business constraints — the global reach and speed of computer-based communications mean distance is only relevant for transferring physical goods.
  • Communicate and share information in many different ways globally — shared files, collaborative documents, wikis (e.g. Wikipedia), video conferencing.
  • Save on communication costs — VoIP (e.g. Skype) costs a fraction of traditional phone calls. Documents can be shared electronically for free.
  • Interact socially on a world-wide scale — social networking (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, SnapChat, Pinterest) and blogs allow global social connections.

VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol)

Technology that allows voice calls to be made over the internet at a fraction of the cost of traditional telephone calls. Examples: Skype, WhatsApp calls.

Types of Computer Systems

Comparing Computers

When comparing computers, consider:

  • How powerful they are — CPU speed, RAM
  • What they are used for — servers vs office computers
  • Their size (mobility) — mainframes to smartphones
  • The software they use — "platforms" (Linux, Windows, macOS)

Non-Portable Computers

Generally consist of a system unit, screen, mouse and keyboard. Large in size, not meant to be moved, generally more powerful than portable computers.

  • Desktop computer: The standard non-portable choice. Available in various configurations and price points.
  • Server: A powerful computer that provides resources and services to all computers linked in a network.

Entry-Level Computer

  • The cheapest model in a range, with fairly low hardware specifications.
  • Often installed with Home Editions of office suites and operating systems (fewer features than Professional versions).
  • Specifications change over time — today's top-level computer will be an entry-level system in a few years.

Portable Computers

Uses a traditional OS (Windows, Linux, macOS) with an all-in-one design: screen, keyboard, pointing device, and battery. Designed for portability.

Laptop / Notebook Screen > 12”–13” Can match high-end desktop specs but costs more

Smartphones / Tablets / Phablets

  • Designed to be always on and always connected
  • Even with blank screen, they are in standby mode
  • Built-in cellular connection (24/7 internet)

Laptops

  • Designed to be switched on, used, then switched off
  • Rely on WiFi or cable for internet
  • No built-in cellular connection

Categories of Users

Personal (Casual) Users

Don't depend heavily on computers. Use them for:

  • Limited office tasks, internet banking, email, web browsing
  • Social networking, Skype, viewing/sharing photos
  • Entertainment: casual gaming, music, movies

Even the most basic entry-level computer handles all these tasks.

SOHO (Small Office Home Office) Users

  • Use computers for business admin: accounting, billing, databases, document archiving, planning/scheduling
  • Some SOHO users (architects, engineers) may need more powerful computers
  • Generally need an entry-level to mid-range computer

Power Users

  • Need top-of-the-range hardware and software
  • Examples: hardcore gamers, architects, engineers, video editors, scientists
  • Need fast processors, large amounts of memory and storage
  • Use all advanced functions of their software

Mobile Users

  • Not a separate category — can be any of the above
  • Need their technology wherever they go
  • Laptops for productive/work tasks; smartphones/tablets for browsing, email, GPS, photos, notes

Convergence

Definition

Convergence is the merging or combining of multiple technologies into a single, multi-purpose device.

The Smartphone as Convergence Example

A smartphone combines the functions of many separate devices:

  • Audio recorder
  • Gaming console
  • Computer
  • GPS navigator
  • Book / magazine / newspaper reader
  • Video camera
  • Camera
  • Email and web browsing
  • Music player / video viewer
  • Telephone / cellphone / videophone

Key Exam Point

Don't confuse convergence (merging technologies into one device) with compatibility (different systems working together). They are different concepts.

Module 1.2 — Hardware Devices

The Information Processing Cycle

Computers take data and process it into information. The cycle has five stages:

StageDescriptionExample Devices
InputProvides data to be processedKeyboard, touch screen, scanner, digital camera
ProcessingSteps, decisions and calculations to convert data into informationCPU, RAM
OutputCommunicates results to people or other devicesScreen, printer, speakers, data projector
StorageKeeps data, software and results when not in use / computer offSSD, flash drive, optical media
CommunicationConnects ICT devices with each otherCellular modem, ADSL router, fibre router

Key Points About Processing

  • Processing can only take place when software AND data are loaded into RAM.
  • Processing uses a step-by-step solution called an algorithm.
  • Typical processing tasks: searching, sorting, comparisons/decisions, mathematical calculations.

Key Points About Input & Output

  • Don't assume input comes from a single source or output goes to a single destination — most systems use multiple input sources and output destinations.
  • Output can also be: sent to storage, communicated to other computers, used as input for other programs, or used to control equipment.

Keyboard & Mouse

Keyboard

AdvantagesLimitations

Easy to learn and use Takes up space; on-screen keyboards used on mobile
Shortcut keys speed up work (e.g. Ctrl+S) Difficult for users with disabilities or arthritis

Mouse

AdvantagesLimitations
Easy to learn; effective for GUI interaction Needs a fixed workplace with clear surface area
Click, double-click, right-click, scroll, drag Entry-level mice are not precise for graphics work
Troubleshooting: Sticky/jumpy pointer → clean, smooth, non-reflective surface. Not responding → replace batteries, reconnect, try different port.

Ergonomics (Keyboards & Mice)

  • Ergonomics: The science of designing equipment to fit the user's physical needs, reducing strain and injury.
  • Ergonomic keyboards position keys in a more natural hand position.
  • Ergonomic/vertical mice keep the hand relaxed and naturally positioned.
  • Reduces the risk of RSI (Repetitive Strain Injury) such as Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.
  • Wrist rests and soft-touch keys can also help.

Wireless

  • Reduces desktop clutter
  • Can't use if batteries die or dongle is lost/damaged

Cabled

  • No batteries needed
  • Always works (as long as connected)

Scanner & Digital Camera

Scanner

  • Uses: Capturing images from paper, converting text via OCR, reading barcodes.
  • Office automation: Multi-page documents → single PDF. Digital storage saves physical space. Scan and email instead of fax/post.
  • POS/Database: Barcode scanners for quick, accurate input in shops and libraries.
  • Other: E-toll plate scanning, access control (swipe cards, RFID), biometric input (fingerprint scanners).

OCR (Optical Character Recognition)

Software that converts scanned images of text into editable digital text. OCR does not scan — the scanner captures the image, then OCR processes it.

  1. Breaks the image into blocks of individual letters.
  2. Compares letter shapes to a database of known letter shapes.
  3. Uses spacing to identify which letters form words.
  4. Searches a dictionary to verify the word exists.
  5. Adds the word to the text document, or flags it for the user to check.

Digital Camera

  • Uses: Capturing images and video, alternative to scanners for documents, barcode/QR code reading (on smartphones).
  • Photos are already digital — no scanning needed.
  • Easy to share, especially from smartphones (auto-upload to Instagram, Facebook).
  • Geotagging: GPS location (latitude and longitude) stored in photo metadata.
  • DSLR cameras produce better images than point-and-shoot due to larger sensors and more manual control.

Resolution & Image Quality

DeviceResolution MeasureKey Values
Scannerdpi (dots per inch)300 dpi for print, 72 dpi for screen/web
CameraMegapixels (H × V pixels)e.g. 2048 × 1536 ≈ 3 megapixels

Image quality factors:

  • Colour depth: Number of bits per colour (max 32 bits). Higher = better.
  • Sensor size: Bigger sensor = better quality. DSLR > point-and-shoot.
  • ISO rating: Sensor sensitivity to light. Higher ISO = better in low light.

Webcam & Microphone

Webcam

  • Uses: Video communication, live feeds, security cameras
  • Advantages: Cheap, small, often built into laptops
  • Limitations: Low resolution, must be connected to a device

Troubleshooting: Check if plugged in, switched on, or selected as current camera in software.

Microphone

  • Uses: Record sound, voice commands, VoIP/video conferencing
  • Advantages: Record meetings/interviews, voice control, add sound input
  • Limitations: Built-in mics have low quality; difficult in noisy environments

Troubleshooting: Plugged into wrong jack (headphone instead of mic), or set on mute.

Monitor / Display

Uses & Advantages

  • Provides immediate, interactive feedback to the user.
  • Content can be changed easily; brightness, contrast and colour settings are adjustable.
  • Text can be enlarged for visually impaired users; high-contrast modes available.
  • Visual alerts can replace audio alerts for hearing-impaired users.

Display Quality Factors

FactorWhat It MeansRule
ResolutionH × V pixels (e.g. 1920 × 1080)Higher = sharper
Colour depthBits per pixel (e.g. 24-bit)More bits = more colours
Pixel density (dpi)Pixels per inchHigher = crisper, clearer
Refresh rateHow often the screen refreshesHigher = less ghosting
Contrast ratioDarkest black vs brightest whiteHigher = sharper, more vibrant
Aspect ratioWidth : height (e.g. 16:9)16:9 modern widescreen; 4:3 older

ProblemSolution

Speakers, Headphones & Printers

Speakers & Headphones

  • Uses: Play sound, audible feedback, accessibility (text-to-speech), VoIP communication.
  • Headphones: private listening, no disturbance to others, mobile use.
  • Speakers: group listening.
  • Troubleshooting: Wrong jack, not powered on, volume on mute, wrong output device selected.

Environmental Impact of Printing

  • Paper manufacturing uses lots of energy and produces a large carbon footprint.
  • Paper manufacturing uses poisonous chemicals harmful to the environment.
  • Ink/toner cartridges and old printers contain chemicals that can seep into ground water.

Reduce impact: Print only when necessary, print double-sided, print 2 pages per sheet, use lower resolution for drafts, recycle printers and cartridges properly.

Social Implications

Security Risks: Malware

TypeDefinition
KeyloggerSoftware that records every key you press to steal passwords and personal data. It is a type of spyware.
SpywareSoftware designed to monitor and record user activity without knowledge or permission.
TrojanMalware disguised as useful software (e.g. a screensaver) that secretly contains a keylogger or other harmful code.
MalwareThe general umbrella term for all malicious software, including keyloggers, spyware, Trojans, viruses, etc.
A keylogger is spyware, NOT a virus. When hidden inside useful-looking software, it's called a Trojan. All fall under the umbrella term malware.

Accuracy & Validity of Data

  • People make mistakes — entered data can be inaccurate or invalid.
  • Time must be spent checking accuracy both manually and through software.
  • Data validation methods: input masks, drop-down lists, date pickers.
