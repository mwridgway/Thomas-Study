Unit 1: Mid-Latitude Cyclones

Overview & Location

What are Mid-Latitude Cyclones?

  • Large-scale low-pressure weather systems that form in the mid-latitudes (30°–60° N and S) of the equator.
  • They form at the polar front where warm westerly winds and cold polar easterly winds converge at the sub-polar low pressure.
  • They occur in families of 3 to 5, which follow one after each other, two to eight days apart.
  • The cold front of an older depression is joined to the warm front of a younger depression.

Cyclogenesis

The development or strengthening of cyclonic circulation in the atmosphere.

Path of Mid-Latitude Cyclones

  • Travel from east to west (actually west to east — carried in the belt of the westerly winds).
  • Initially move towards the equator, then curve back towards the poles.
  • This is due to the faster-moving, more forceful cold front and their deflection by the subtropical high pressures.

Areas Where Mid-Latitude Cyclones Form

  • Occur in the mid-latitudes (30° to 60° north and south of the equator).
  • Form at the polar front where warm westerly winds and cold polar easterly winds converge at the sub-polar low pressure.

Atmospheric Setup (Southern Hemisphere)

Conditions Necessary for Formation

The conditions favourable for the formation of a mid-latitude cyclone are:

  1. Air from the two large high-pressure belts moves towards each other at the polar front.
  2. Warm subtropical HP contains a warm, moist air mass. Polar HP contains cold, dry air mass.
  3. Air flow converges from opposite directions on the two sides of the polar front.
  4. Disturbances at the polar front result in an unstable situation, creating a low pressure (into which air will flow and around which air will circulate).

Formation Zone

The area of possible cyclone formation lies between the warm subtropical HP (with warm moist westerly winds) and the cold polar HP (with cold easterly winds), along the polar front where a low-pressure trough develops.

Movement of Air Within a Mid-Latitude Cyclone

  • Air rotates clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere and anti-clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere around the centre of a low pressure.
  • Warm air rises at the cold front and at the warm front.

Stages in the Development of Mid-Latitude Cyclones

(A) Initial Stage

  • Warm, moist subtropical air meets cold, drier polar air along a stationary polar front.
  • No movement across the polar front.
  • Air masses don't mix, but move parallel to each other.
  • Both air masses retain their own density and characteristics.
Isobars: ~1 006–1 008 hPa, parallel lines

(B) Development or Wave Stage

A front has a wave-like appearance.

  • A disturbance occurs at the polar front due to one of the following factors:
    • Acceleration in the movement of one of the two air masses
    • Disturbances in the westerly wind belt
    • Uneven surfaces or the shape of the coastline
    • Differences between land and sea temperatures
  • This results in a local decrease in air pressure.
  • A tongue of warm, less dense air is uplifted by cold, dense air, and consequently a LP starts to form.
Isobars: ~1 004–1 008 hPa, beginning to bend

(C) Mature or Warm Sector Stage

  • Cold air starts to move in a northerly direction, west of the LP centre — a cold front develops.
  • Warm air is pushed in a southerly direction, east of the LP — warm front develops.
  • Wind blows clockwise around and into the LP (Southern Hemisphere).
  • Warm air rises above cold, heavier air.
  • Air masses don't mix — separated by fronts.
  • A definite warm sector depression develops.
  • Isobars bend across the fronts.
  • Rains along cold front — strong uplift of warm air.
Isobars: ~1 006–1 008 hPa, with distinct kinks at fronts

(D) Occluded Stage

  • Warm sector narrows as development continues.
  • Cold front moves faster than warm front, because warm front is preceded (slowed down by cold air ahead of it).
  • Cold front starts to overtake the warm front.

(E) Dissipation Stage

  • Cold air completely isolates warm air from the ground.
  • All warm air has been lifted high above ground.
  • Isobars become more even as the LP degenerates.
  • Upper level winds break apart the cloud cover.
  • Rainfall decreases and eventually stops.

Wind Direction in Mid-Latitude Cyclones

As the system passes over, the wind changes direction as a result of the air circulating clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere and anti-clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere.

Southern Hemisphere

The winds 'back' from northeasterly to northwesterly to south-westerly.

Wind direction changes according to an anticlockwise pattern as the warm front (1), warm sector (2), and cold front (3) passes.

  1. NE wind (before warm front)
  2. NW wind (warm sector)
  3. SW wind (after cold front)

Northern Hemisphere

The winds 'veer' from southeasterly to southwesterly to north-easterly.

Wind direction changes according to a clockwise pattern as the warm front (1), warm sector (2), and cold front (3) passes.

  1. SE wind (before warm front)
  2. SW wind (warm sector)
  3. NE wind (after cold front)

Occluded Fronts

Cold Front Occlusion

Air behind the cold front is colder and therefore denser than the air ahead of the warm front.

The cold front remains in contact with the ground, and lifts the warm front.

Warm Front Occlusion

Air behind the cold front is warmer and therefore lighter than the air ahead of the warm front.

The warm front remains in contact with the ground, and the cold front rises.

In both cases, the warm air in the warm sector is forced to rise. As it cools, condensation occurs and rain falls from nimbostratus clouds.

Impact of Mid-Latitude Cyclones on Human Activities

  • Winter rainfall in Western Cape — cultivation of crops — profitable for the wine industry.
  • Rain brought by frontal depressions replenishes water in dams.
  • Snow falls on Cape Fold Mountains — danger to livestock.
  • Very cold conditions experienced when cold front crosses the plateau and moves into high veld — power cuts.
  • Berg winds and hot, dry conditions are experienced ahead of cold front — fires.
  • SW winds behind the cold front — stormy and/or gale-force winds over ocean — danger to ships.
  • Heavy rainfall from cold front passing the Western Cape — poor visibility.
  • Mountain passes may be closed if there is snow.

Unit 2: Tropical Cyclones

What is a Tropical Cyclone?

A tropical cyclone is a cell of very low pressure with a steep pressure gradient, strong winds and heavy rain.

Names in Different Regions

NameRegion
HurricanesAtlantic Ocean: West Indies, Gulf of Mexico, south and east coast of USA
Willy-williesSouthern Pacific Ocean: Eastern Australia
TyphoonsNorthern Pacific Ocean: China, Japan and Philippines
Tropical cyclonesIndian Ocean: East coast of Southern Africa and Madagascar

General Characteristics

  • Intense low pressure systems.
  • Originate in late summer or autumn over warm, tropical oceans (at least 26°C).
  • Need Coriolis force to form (which is zero within 5° of the equator) — develop within latitude 5° to 20° N and S.
  • Move westwards at a rate of 40–200 km a day. Near 20° to 30° N and S, they tend to curve back eastwards at 500–700 km a day.
  • As low pressure areas: rotation is clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere and anti-clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere.
  • Energy source: large amount of latent heat released as rising warm, moist tropical air condenses.
  • Accompanied by heavy rain, storm surges and high winds (200–300 km/h).
  • Diameter of the storm: 500 km.
  • Diameter of the eye: 30–50 km / pressure in the eye: 940 hPa.
  • Pattern of the isobars: circular.
  • Vertical extent of the cloud wall: 15 km.

Path of Tropical Cyclones

  • Develop over tropical oceans ±5° north and south of the equator (no Coriolis force within 5°).
  • Move from east to west (carried by tropical easterly winds) — affect the east coast of continents.
  • They curve to their left in the Southern Hemisphere (away from equator) and to their right in the Northern Hemisphere.
  • As they move further from the equator, they are picked up by westerly winds and travel polewards to 25°–30° north and south.
  • They dissipate when they reach cooler latitudes or as they cross land.

Stages in the Development of Tropical Cyclones

1. Formative Stage

Air pressureLP system develops over sea. Strong convection. Rising air causes LP. Air pressure still above 1 000 hPa, but falls rapidly. Convergence of air and a vortex develops.
TemperatureHigh sea/air temperatures and humidity. Condensation and release of latent heat feed system. The LP starts to deepen.
Cloud coverCirrus and cumulus clouds and light rain occur. Tall vertical clouds develop. Eye of storm now starts to take shape.
WindWinds circulate around the LP — clockwise in SH. Winds in active quadrant reach gale force (8 on Beaufort scale).
AreaSmall area is influenced (20 km from eye).

2. Immature Stage

Air pressureAir pressure in the centre (eye) falls below 1 000 hPa and still decreases. Converging warm, moist air continues to rise → decrease in air pressure in the centre.
TemperatureThe vortex contracts and the storm intensifies as heat continues to feed the system. Wind speeds increase with more evaporation and condensation, releasing latent heat.
Cloud coverSpiral bands of clouds form and light rain occurs. Cumulonimbus clouds surround the eye, reaching height of up to 12 km.
WindDivergence of air takes place in the upper air. Hurricane wind speeds within 50 km of eye and gales within 500 km of eye.
AreaStorm is still small in size, but a larger area is influenced (600 km in diameter).

3. Mature Stage

Air pressureAir pressure in eye reaches lowest point, about 900–940 hPa or lower. Pressure will cease to drop when balance between upper air divergence and surface convergence occurs.
TemperatureWarm, moist air still converges at the surface. The unstable air rises around centre of the low. Air sinks into the centre of storm and warms adiabatically.
Cloud coverHeavy rain from cumulonimbus clouds within 160 km from eye.
WindHurricane strength winds (about 117 km/h) have extended to 300 km from eye. Upper air divergence increases. More stable conditions exist in the eye.
AreaSize of storm increases. Worst weather in the active quadrant. The mature stage can last up to 7 days.

4. Degenerating Stage

Air pressurePressure in the eye rises when land is reached as friction weakens Coriolis force and air converges on the low pressure.
TemperatureTemperatures decrease when the cyclone moves into cooler latitudes and cold air from poles is fed into system.
Cloud coverLess moisture is fed in the system and latent heat released, but heavy rains continue to fall.
WindWinds decrease in speed. Upper air divergence stops.
AreaArea affected shrinks as tropical cyclone dissipates.

Reading and Interpreting Satellite Images

How to Identify a Tropical Cyclone on a Synoptic Map

  • A low-pressure cell with circular isobars between 5° and 30° N/S of the equator.
  • A tropical cyclone symbol in the eye.
  • The name of the tropical cyclone written next to the system.
  • The date on the map in late summer or early autumn.

Impact of Tropical Cyclones on Human Activities and the Environment

Impacts Flow Chart

A tropical cyclone causes: Heavy rain → Flooding → Contamination of water supply → Human injuries, disease, deaths. High wind velocity → Storm surge → Damage to structures, vegetation, crops, livestock → Damage to shipping. Low central pressure → Storm surge → Flooding of low-lying coastal lands → Coastal erosion → Saline intrusion.

Developed Areas

  • Suffer more from financial losses due to reduced trade and huge insurance claims.
  • Financial, technology and communication resources are more available in MEDCs to assist with warnings, evacuations and relief.

Developing Areas

  • Suffer more from basic problems such as loss of homes and insufficient food and water.
  • Greater loss of life in LEDCs due to lack of financial, technological and communication resources.

General Impacts

  • Flooding of rivers due to heavy rain — damage to roads, bridges and homes.
  • High sea level and high waves — flooding in low-lying, often densely populated coastal areas.
  • Strong winds and floods damage crops, livestock, houses, infrastructure.
  • Landslides destroy crops, settlements and infrastructure.
  • Communication lines destroyed.
  • Tourism at island resorts is affected.
  • Fertile soil is flooded and washed into the sea.
  • Trees and vegetation are destroyed — loss of habitat for birds and animals.
  • Drowning, death, disease especially in developing regions and starvation.

Strategies to Prepare and Manage the Effects of Tropical Cyclones

Immediate Precautions

  • Avoid crossing strongly-flowing or flooded rivers.
  • Dwellings very close to rivers or floodplains should be evacuated in good time.
  • Farmers should move pumps away from rivers.
  • Small to medium-sized boats and fishing crafts should return to port.
  • Should be moored well above the normal high-water mark.

Monitoring and Warning

  • Tropical storms and depressions are monitored via weather bureaus, reconnaissance aircraft and satellite photographs.
  • Inhabitants are warned to vacate low ground, board up windows and move to concrete storm shelters.
  • Relief schemes, evacuation procedures and disaster management schemes must be in operation.

Long-term Strategies

  • Sand bags can be placed in coastal areas to reduce flooding by the sea.
  • Countries can reduce the damaging effects of future tropical cyclones by erecting concrete sea walls and flood barriers along rivers.
  • Concentrate on better land-use planning to ensure the maintenance and conservation of wetlands and coastal vegetation, which often buffer against storm surges.
  • In the long term, countries that frequently experience this hazard need to focus on areas along rivers for flood protection.
